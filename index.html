A Day in the Life of a Data Scientist in Financial Services

Insights from Sudeshna Banerjee at the Monday Seminar

March 17, 2025

On March 17, 2025, I had the privilege of attending a Monday Seminar titled A Day in the Life of a Data Scientist in Financial Services, led by Sudeshna Banerjee, the Head of AI and Innovation Modeling at Wells Fargo. This event offered a deep dive into the evolving role of data scientists within the financial services industry, and I walked away with a wealth of knowledge that I’m eager to apply.

The Evolution of the Data Scientist Role

Sudeshna kicked off the seminar by discussing the historical shift in the data scientist role. In the past, data scientists were often considered “grunts,” performing labor-intensive tasks that were mostly technical and focused on executing pre-defined models. Today, however, the role has evolved significantly. Data scientists are no longer just technicians; they have become leaders, guides, and strategic partners who manage the full lifecycle of data science projects—from data collection to model deployment and everything in between. This shift in responsibility underscores the need for a well-rounded skill set that encompasses both the technical and the collaborative aspects of data science.

Model Development Life Cycle (MDLC) and LLMs

One of the standout topics from the seminar was the Model Development Life Cycle (MDLC) and the increasing importance of Large Language Models (LLMs) in financial services. Sudeshna explained that data scientists now work with pre-made and approved models, which allows them to move quickly and focus on more complex problem-solving. This change highlights the transition from building models from the ground up to using existing frameworks and improving upon them. By understanding the MDLC and leveraging LLMs, data scientists can ensure more efficient and scalable solutions.

Collaboration and Storytelling in Data Science

Another key takeaway was the growing emphasis on communication and collaboration within the data science field. Sudeshna emphasized that data science is not just about building models—it’s about ensuring those models are understood, deployed effectively, and integrated across various teams. Communication with both technical teams and non-technical stakeholders is vital. The ability to tell a compelling story with data is becoming increasingly important, as data scientists must present complex findings in a clear, actionable way. This storytelling aspect adds significant value to the technical work and helps bridge the gap between data science and business strategy.

Tech Stacks and Continuous Learning

Finally, Sudeshna discussed the importance of mastering diverse tech stacks to be successful in today’s data science environment. From programming languages like Python and R to cloud computing platforms and machine learning frameworks, data scientists must be equipped with a robust set of tools to stay ahead in the fast-paced world of financial services.

In conclusion, this seminar was an eye-opening experience. It highlighted how the role of a data scientist has evolved from being highly technical and task-oriented to a more strategic, guiding position. Sudeshna Banerjee’s insights were not only thought-provoking but also motivating, making me excited for the future of data science and the opportunities to contribute to the innovation shaping industries like financial services.
