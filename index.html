A Day in the Life of a Data Scientist in Financial Services

Insights from Sudeshna Banerjee at UNC Charlottte Seminar

By Tommie Clark

>

March 17, 2025

On March 17, 2025, I had the privilege of attending a Monday Seminar titled A Day in the Life of a Data Scientist in Financial Services, led by Sudeshna Banerjee, the Head of AI and Innovation Modeling at Wells Fargo. This event offered a deep dive into the evolving role of data scientists within the financial services industry, and I walked away with a wealth of knowledge that I’m eager to apply.

Sudeshna kicked off the seminar by discussing the historical shift in the data scientist role. In the past, data scientists were often considered “grunts,” performing labor-intensive tasks that were mostly technical and focused on executing pre-defined models. Today, however, the role has evolved significantly. Data scientists are no longer just technicians; they have become leaders, guides, and strategic partners who manage the full lifecycle of data science projects—from data collection to model deployment and everything in between. This shift in responsibility underscores the need for a well-rounded skill set that encompasses both the technical and the collaborative aspects of data science.

One of the standout topics from the seminar was the Model Development Life Cycle (MDLC), which guides data scientists through model creation, testing, deployment, and monitoring. Sudeshna explained that, given the highly regulated nature of the financial services industry, while some pre-approved models are used, the majority of model development still requires careful customization and validation. Ready-made models can be used as frameworks, but there’s always an extra layer of compliance and scrutiny when it comes to implementing them in such a highly regulated environment.

Another major change discussed was how the work of data scientists has moved from traditional tools like Excel to more powerful platforms. These platforms enable better monitoring of policies, governance, and the full lifecycle of data science projects. The shift not only enhances collaboration but also ensures that everything remains compliant with industry regulations. This move has allowed teams to be more efficient, trackable, and transparent in their work, facilitating faster decision-making while adhering to regulatory requirements.

Sudeshna also touched on the concept of the data lifespan. In the world of financial services, projects that are no longer in use or deemed irrelevant are purged from development. This process helps streamline resources and ensures that data storage is used efficiently. It's a reminder of how important it is for data scientists to continuously evaluate the utility and impact of their models and projects. If a project isn't being used or doesn’t contribute to business goals, it’s often removed to maintain a lean and effective data environment.

In conclusion, this seminar was an eye-opening experience. It highlighted how the role of a data scientist has evolved from being highly technical and task-oriented to a more strategic, guiding position. Sudeshna Banerjee’s insights were not only thought-provoking but also motivating, making me excited for the future of data science and the opportunities to contribute to the innovation shaping industries like financial services.
